================================================================
  PLAIN-LANGUAGE EXPLANATION OF ALL 5 SUMMARY PLOTS
  Project: Religion-Conditioned Emotional Bias in AI Models
  Models: FLAN-T5-Large (Google) | Sarvam-2B (Sarvam AI)
================================================================

These 5 plots answer three core questions:
  (1) Does religion context change the emotion prediction?
  (2) Why does asking the model to explain its reasoning make
      bias so much worse?
  (3) Which specific part of the AI network is responsible?

Each plot is designed to be self-contained. You do not need any
machine learning knowledge to understand them — everything is
explained in everyday language below.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FIGURE 1 — fig1_religion_trigger.png
"Does Mentioning a Religion Change the Emotion the AI Predicts?"
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

WHAT THE PLOT SHOWS:
  Two bar charts side by side — one for T5 (left) and one for
  Sarvam (right). Each chart has four bars, one per religion:
  Hindu, Muslim, Sikh, and Christian.

  The HEIGHT of each bar = the percentage of the 100 test
  situations where the model predicted a DIFFERENT emotion
  just because of the religion label. The situations themselves
  are identical — only the religion word changes.

  Example: A bar at 30% for Christian means:
    "In 30 out of 100 identical situations, T5 predicted a
    different emotion when the situation said Christian
    compared to when there was no religion label."
    This is the bias — the same situation, different treatment.

  The thick horizontal dotted line shows 20% — this is the
  level you would expect from random chance alone (if the model
  was guessing randomly among 5 emotions, it would "change"
  20% of the time). Any bar above this line shows more bias
  than random chance.

HOW TO READ IT:
  Taller bar = more bias for that religion group.
  All bars should ideally be at 0% (religion should not matter).

WHAT THE NUMBERS SHOW:

  FLAN-T5-Large:
    Hindu     → 26% of situations changed emotion
    Muslim    → 21% of situations changed emotion
    Sikh      → 21% of situations changed emotion
    Christian → 30% of situations changed emotion  ← highest
    OVERALL   → 85% of situations showed at least one religion
                causing an emotion change

  Sarvam-2B:
    Hindu     →  5% of situations changed emotion
    Muslim    → 14% of situations changed emotion
    Sikh      →  7% of situations changed emotion
    Christian →  6% of situations changed emotion
    OVERALL   → 90% of situations showed at least one religion
                causing an emotion change

WHY THE PER-RELIGION AND OVERALL NUMBERS DIFFER FOR SARVAM:
  Each religion individually only changed a small fraction of
  situations (5-14%). But across ALL four religions, almost
  EVERY situation showed at least one religion causing a different
  emotion. Think of it like rolling dice: individually each face
  has a small chance of coming up, but roll four dice and you'll
  almost always see at least one of them be different.

KEY INSIGHT:
  Both models are highly biased. An AI that was truly fair would
  show bars at or near 0%. T5's bars are above 20% for all
  religions — consistently worse than random chance. The Christian
  label causes the most emotion changes in T5 (30%), while the
  Muslim label causes the most in Sarvam (14%).

FIGURE 2 — fig2_direct_vs_reasoning.png
"Why Does Asking the AI to Explain Itself Make Bias Worse?"
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

WHAT THE PLOT SHOWS:
  Two pairs of bars — one pair per model. Each pair shows:
    Blue bar  = "Direct mode" bias rate
    Red bar   = "Reasoning mode" bias rate
  A double-headed arrow between the two bars shows the gap.

  BIAS RATE = the percentage of situations where at least one
  religion caused a different emotion prediction.
  A higher bias rate = more bias overall.

WHAT ARE THE TWO MODES?

  Direct mode (blue bar): We ask the model one simple question:
    "What emotion does this person feel?" — Joy, Sadness, Anger,
    Fear, or Neutral. The model answers immediately.

  Reasoning mode (red bar): We first ask the model to write out
    an explanation — "Explain why this person might feel a
    certain way in this situation." Then we ask it to classify
    the emotion based on its OWN explanation.
    This method is known as "chain-of-thought prompting" in AI
    research and is generally thought to improve accuracy.
    But here it amplifies bias instead.

WHAT THE NUMBERS SHOW:

  FLAN-T5-Large:
    Direct mode bias rate    : 42%
    Reasoning mode bias rate : 83%
    Gap                      : +41 percentage points
    → Bias almost DOUBLED when reasoning was added.

  Sarvam-2B:
    Direct mode bias rate    :  16%
    Reasoning mode bias rate :  76%
    Gap                      : +60 percentage points
    → Bias increased almost 5× when reasoning was added.

WHY DOES REASONING MAKE IT WORSE?
  When the model writes an explanation, it uses language like:
    "In a Muslim family, the person might feel..."
    "A Hindu person in this situation would typically..."
  The model has absorbed stereotypes from the internet text it
  was trained on, and writing them out in the explanation locks
  in the biased reasoning. The explanation then DRIVES the
  emotion answer.

  In direct mode, the model goes straight to the answer without
  writing stereotypes explicitly, so there is less opportunity
  for the stereotypes to surface.

  Sarvam shows a bigger gap (+60 points) than T5 (+41 points),
  meaning Sarvam's explanations are more prone to incorporating
  religious stereotypes than T5's.

KEY INSIGHT:
  "Thinking out loud" in AI makes religion bias significantly
  worse. A technique that is supposed to improve AI quality
  (chain-of-thought reasoning) actually makes discrimination
  worse in this context. This is an important and concerning
  finding for AI safety.

FIGURE 3 — fig3_layer_contribution.png
"Which Internal Part of the AI Network Creates the Bias?"
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

WHAT THE PLOT SHOWS:
  Two vertical bar charts (one per model), each showing the
  "contribution score" of every internal layer in the network.

  A neural network like T5 or Sarvam is made of many layers
  stacked on top of each other — like floors in a building.
  Information flows from the bottom floor (layer 0) to the top
  floor (last layer), getting processed at each floor.

  We tested each layer by temporarily "turning it off" — making
  it pass information through unchanged, as if that floor of the
  building did nothing. Then we measured whether bias went up or
  down.

  CONTRIBUTION SCORE (the bar height):
    Positive score (red bar) = turning this layer OFF reduced bias.
      → This layer was CREATING bias. Higher bar = more responsible.
    Negative score (blue bar) = turning this layer OFF made bias WORSE.
      → This layer was FIGHTING bias. It was helping reduce it.
    Bar near zero = this layer has no effect on bias.

HOW TO READ IT:
  - A tall red bar = "this layer is the main culprit"
  - A tall blue bar = "this layer was actually a good guard against bias"
  - Most bars near zero = most layers are not involved in bias

WHAT THE NUMBERS SHOW:

  FLAN-T5-Large (starting bias = 85%):
    Decoder Layer 6  : +83% ← one bar towers over all others
      → Turning off this ONE layer drops bias from 85% to 2%.
        This layer alone is responsible for 83% of T5's bias.
    Encoder Layer 18 : +12% → drops bias to 73%
    Encoder Layer 6  : + 9% → drops bias to 76%
    Decoder Layer 4  : −10% (bias-FIGHTING layer — removing it
                              raises bias to 95%)
    Encoder Layer 0  : − 7% (another bias-fighting layer)

  Sarvam-2B (starting bias = 90%):
    Decoder Layer 0  : +63% ← most responsible layer
      → Turning this off drops bias from 90% to 27%.
    Decoder Layer 12 : +37% → drops bias to 53%
    Decoder Layer 1  : +18% → drops bias to 72%
    Layers 14, 15, 17, 18, 19: −10% each (bias-FIGHTING layers —
      removing any one of these causes bias to jump to 100%!)

KEY INSIGHT:
  T5's bias is like a single bad employee doing most of the
  damage (Decoder Layer 6). Remove that one employee and almost
  all bias disappears.

  Sarvam's bias is like a committee of workers each contributing
  a little (Layers 0, 1, 12, 16). No single removal fixes it.

  Both models also have layers that are ACTIVELY fighting bias.
  The AI is not purely biased — it has internal correction
  mechanisms that are simply being overpowered by the bad layers.

FIGURE 4 — fig4_head_contribution.png
"Which Specific Worker Inside the Biased Layer Is Responsible?"
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

WHAT THE PLOT SHOWS:
  Two heatmaps (colored grids), one per model. Each row shows one
  of the most biased layers found in Figure 3. Each column shows
  one of the 16 "attention heads" within that layer.

  What is an ATTENTION HEAD?
  Each layer in a transformer network contains 16 "workers" called
  attention heads. Each head independently reads the text and
  decides what to focus on. Their outputs are combined to form the
  layer's final result. Think of them as 16 committee members that
  each read the same document and produce their own analysis, then
  vote on the conclusion.

  The color in each cell = what happens to bias when that one
  worker is individually turned off (set to zero):
    RED cell = turning off this head reduces bias (this head was causing it)
    BLUE cell = turning off this head increases bias (this head was helping)
    WHITE/PALE cell = this head has no effect on bias

  The number inside each cell = the exact percentage change.

HOW TO READ IT:
  Look for the most red cells — those are the specific workers
  most responsible for bias. If everything is white/pale, it means
  no single worker is solely responsible (the whole layer cooperates).

WHAT THE NUMBERS SHOW:

  FLAN-T5-Large:
    All cells are near 0% — no single head stands out.
    → T5's bias in Decoder Layer 6 is a GROUP effort. All 16 heads
      work together to create it. You cannot fix the problem by
      removing just one head — the entire layer needs to be changed.
    → The negative cells in Decoder Layer 4 heads confirm that
      layer is collectively suppressing bias (all heads help fight it).

  Sarvam-2B:
    Decoder Layer 12, Head 1 : −15% (most red cell)
      → Turning off this ONE worker alone reduces total bias from
        90% to 75%. This is the most specific "bias culprit" we found.
    Decoder Layer 0,  Heads 5, 10, 11 : −10% each
    Decoder Layer 12, Heads 0, 5      : −10% each
    Decoder Layer 16, Heads 0, 7, 12  : −10% each
    → A handful of specific workers across three layers are identified.
      Notably: turning off these heads eliminates bias in DIRECT MODE
      completely, while reasoning-mode bias remains — confirming these
      specific workers are responsible only for direct-mode bias.

KEY INSIGHT:
  The precision of the finding differs between the models:
  T5  : "Decoder Layer 6 as a WHOLE causes the bias — a building-level
         problem, not a single worker."
  Sarvam: "Decoder Layer 12, Head 1 is the single most responsible
           individual worker — a targeted fix might be possible."

FIGURE 5 — fig5_probing_when_encoded.png
"At Which Point Does the AI First Become Aware of the Religion?"
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

WHAT THE PLOT SHOWS:
  Line charts with lines going from left (first layer) to right
  (last layer), showing the accuracy of a simple detector.

  WHAT IS THE DETECTOR?
  We trained a very simple linear model (a logistic regression)
  on the internal signals from each layer of the AI, asking it
  two questions:
    (A) Can you predict the EMOTION (Joy/Sadness/Anger/Fear/Neutral)
        that the model will output, just from the internal signal
        at this layer?
    (B) Can you predict the RELIGION (None/Hindu/Muslim/Sikh/
        Christian) that was in the prompt, just from the internal
        signal at this layer?

  High accuracy = the information IS present in the internal
    signal at that layer (the detector can find it)
  Low accuracy ≈ 20% = the information is NOT present
    (20% is random chance for 5 categories)

  The dotted line at 20% is the random chance baseline.
  Any line clearly above 20% means the AI model "knows" that
  information at that layer.

HOW TO READ IT:
  - A JUMP in a line = that layer is where new information
    suddenly becomes available in the model's internal signals.
  - A FLAT line above 20% = the information was already there
    and stays throughout (no single "entry point").
  - A line flat at 20% = the information is absent at those layers.

WHAT THE NUMBERS SHOW:

  FLAN-T5-Large Encoder (processes the written prompt):
    Religion detector accuracy: 61% from layer 0 → stays 60-63%
      → The AI knows the religion from the VERY FIRST LAYER of
        processing the input. It never forgets it.
    Emotion detector accuracy: 52% throughout
      → Weak emotion signal throughout the encoder — the input
        text contains hints of emotion but the final answer is
        not decided yet.

  FLAN-T5-Large Decoder (generates the output word):
    Religion detector at layer 0: 18% ← below random chance!
      → At the very start of the decoder, there is ZERO religion
        information (even less than random guessing).
    Religion detector at layer 1: 60% ← SUDDEN JUMP
      → In one step, the decoder goes from knowing nothing to
        knowing everything about the religion. This jump happens
        because layer 1 is where the decoder reads from the encoder.
    Emotion detector: same pattern — 26% at layer 0, jumps to 47% at layer 1.

  Sarvam-2B Decoder (does everything in one pass):
    Religion detector: 66% at layer 0 → stays 66-69% throughout
    Emotion detector:  70% at layer 0 → stays 70-73% throughout
    → Sarvam has even HIGHER detection accuracy than T5, from the
      very first layer. No jump, no entry point — religion and
      emotion information are present from the absolute beginning
      and maintained across all 28 layers.

KEY INSIGHT:
  Religion information is NOT gradually introduced as the model
  processes — it is there from the VERY FIRST LAYER and stays
  forever. This means:

  1. Bias is not a "late stage" problem — it is embedded in the
     model's understanding of the text from the start.
  2. You cannot fix bias by only modifying the last few layers.
     The religion signal flows through the ENTIRE network.
  3. The consistently high religion detection accuracy (60-69%
     across ALL layers, far above 20% random chance) confirms
     that no layer "forgets" the religion — it is always available
     to be acted upon by whatever layer creates the bias.

  Think of it like a person who reads "In a Hindu family..." and
  never stops being aware of that fact throughout their thinking
  process. The religion tag follows the model's "train of thought"
  from beginning to end.

================================================================
HOW ALL 5 PLOTS TELL ONE COMPLETE STORY
================================================================

  Fig 1 answers WHAT:
    Religion context changes the predicted emotion in 85-90% of
    situations. Christian context is most potent for T5, Muslim
    for Sarvam.

  Fig 2 answers WHY IT IS WORSE WITH REASONING:
    Asking the model to explain its thinking before answering
    forces it to write out religious stereotypes explicitly,
    which then determine the emotion answer. This increases bias
    by +41 percentage points for T5 and +60 points for Sarvam.

  Fig 3 answers WHERE (which layer):
    T5: Decoder Layer 6 alone is responsible for 83% of bias.
    Sarvam: Decoder Layers 0 and 12 are the primary contributors.
    Both models also have "good" layers that fight bias.

  Fig 4 answers WHICH (which specific worker in the layer):
    T5: No single worker — the whole of Decoder Layer 6 cooperates.
    Sarvam: Decoder Layer 12, Head 1 is the most identifiable
    single responsible worker (accounts for 15% of total bias).

  Fig 5 answers WHEN (how early does the model notice religion):
    Religion is noticed at the VERY FIRST LAYER in both models
    and is never forgotten. Bias is not introduced late —
    it is woven in from the moment the model reads the input text.

================================================================
STUDY SETUP (for reference)
================================================================
  - 100 different situations across 4 topics: family, workplace,
    legal, and general everyday life
  - 5 religion conditions: no religion, Hindu, Muslim, Sikh, Christian
  - 2 ways of asking: direct question and reasoning-first question
  - 5 possible emotions: Joy, Sadness, Anger, Fear, Neutral
  - Total predictions made: 1000 per model (100 × 5 × 2)
  - Each ablation experiment: re-ran all 1000 predictions
    with one layer/head turned off
