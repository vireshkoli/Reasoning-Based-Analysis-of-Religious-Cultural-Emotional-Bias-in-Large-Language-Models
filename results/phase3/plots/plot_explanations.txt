These plots come from Phase 3 of the study, which goes INSIDE the
neural network to understand HOW and WHERE religion-based bias is
created. Think of it like doing an MRI of the model's brain to find
which specific part causes the biased behavior.

================================================================
QUICK BACKGROUND: KEY CONCEPTS
================================================================

HIDDEN STATE:
  Every transformer layer takes in a vector of numbers (the hidden
  state) and outputs a modified version. This vector "represents"
  what the model understands at that point. We can analyze these
  vectors to understand what the model is "thinking" at each layer.

LAYER ABLATION (BYPASSING A LAYER):
  We "turn off" a layer by making it output exactly what it received
  as input (as if the layer did nothing). If the bias disappears
  after turning off a layer, that layer was causing the bias.

PROBING CLASSIFIER:
  A simple linear model trained on the hidden states from one layer
  to predict either the emotion or the religion. If it can predict
  religion with 70% accuracy, that layer "encodes" religion information.
  Random chance = 20% (5 classes).

ATTENTION:
  At each layer, the model "pays attention" to different parts of
  the input. Attention entropy measures how SPREAD OUT this attention
  is — high entropy = attention spread everywhere, low entropy =
  attention focused on just a few words.

RTAS (Religion Token Attention Score):
  For each attention head, how much does it focus specifically on
  the religion words (e.g., "Hindu", "Muslim") in the prompt?
  High RTAS = that head is a "religion detector" head.

CKA (Centered Kernel Alignment):
  A number from 0 to 1 measuring how similar two sets of hidden
  states are. CKA=1 means the representations are identical.
  CKA=0 means completely different. Used to compare T5 and Sarvam.

LSS (Layer-wise Sensitivity Score):
  For each layer, how much do the hidden states CHANGE when religion
  is injected vs. not injected? Higher LSS = this layer is more
  affected by the religion context.

LBS (Layer-wise Bias Score):
  Does this layer treat religions DIFFERENTLY? High LBS means the
  layer produces very different hidden states for Hindu vs. Muslim
  vs. Sikh vs. Christian prompts (differential treatment).

PLOT 1 — lss_curves_all.png
"How Much Does Religion Change the Model Internally? (LSS)"
================================================================

WHAT THE PLOT SHOWS:
  Three side-by-side panels. Each panel has multiple colored lines
  (one per religion: Hindu, Muslim, Sikh, Christian) plus an average
  (black dashed) showing the Layer-wise Sensitivity Score (LSS).

  The x-axis runs from 0 to 1 (normalized layer depth — 0 = first
  layer, 1 = last layer). This lets you compare T5 and Sarvam on
  the same scale even though they have different numbers of layers.
  The y-axis is the LSS value — how much the hidden state shifts.

  Panel 1 = T5 Encoder (processes the input prompt)
  Panel 2 = T5 Decoder (generates the output)
  Panel 3 = Sarvam Decoder (processes everything in one pass)

HOW TO READ IT:
  - A peak at a certain layer = religion changes the internal
    representation most at that layer.
  - A flat line = the effect of religion is uniform across layers.
  - If lines for different religions are far apart = different
    religions affect the model differently.
  - The formula is:
    LSS = (distance between "religion" hidden state and "no religion"
           hidden state) / (size of "no religion" hidden state)
    So LSS=0.3 means the religion-injected hidden state is 30%
    "farther away" from baseline than the baseline's own length.

WHAT THE NUMBERS SHOW:

  T5 ENCODER (Panel 1):
    Layer 0 (first): LSS = 0.29 — immediately affected by religion
    Layers 1-7: drops from 0.26 → 0.17 (effect diminishing)
    Layers 8-22: plateau at ~0.12 (low but persistent background effect)
    Layer 24 (last): 0.21 (slight increase — embedding layer output)
    → Religion context enters at the very first layer and stays
      throughout, but its direct impact decreases after early layers.

  T5 DECODER (Panel 2):
    Layer 0 (first): LSS = 0.45 — highest of all! The very first
      decoder step is most strongly affected.
    Layer 1: drops to 0.30
    Layer 7: drops to 0.06
    Layers 8-23: flat at 0.04-0.06 (low but nonzero)
    → The decoder picks up the religion signal strongly at the start
      (from the encoder's output) then processes it away quickly.

  SARVAM DECODER (Panel 3):
    Layer 0 (first): LSS = 0.23
    Layer 1: drops sharply to 0.04
    Layers 2-6: rises slightly to 0.04-0.06
    Layers 7-16: gradually rises to ~0.07-0.08
    Layer 28 (last): 0.02 (drops at the very end)
    → Sarvam's sensitivity is lower and more evenly distributed
      than T5's decoder. No single layer dominates.

KEY INSIGHT:
  Both models are most sensitive to religion at the very first layer.
  Religion information enters immediately and is not introduced
  gradually. T5's decoder shows the strongest single-layer peak
  (LSS=0.45 at layer 0). Sarvam's sensitivity is more spread out.
  The per-religion lines being close together means all four
  religions cause similar AMOUNTS of change (just different
  DIRECTIONS — that's what LBS measures next).

PLOT 2 — lbs_curves_all.png
"Does the Model Treat Different Religions Differently? (LBS)"
================================================================

WHAT THE PLOT SHOWS:
  Same three-panel layout as LSS. Each panel shows the Layer-wise
  Bias Score (LBS) — the variance (spread) in how much the hidden
  states change across religions at each layer.

  Think of it this way:
  - LSS asked: "How much does religion change things overall?"
  - LBS asks: "Is the change DIFFERENT for each religion?"
  - High LBS = the model is treating Hindu differently from Muslim,
    differently from Christian, etc. (this IS the bias)

HOW TO READ IT:
  - A flat low line = all religions cause similar shifts (equal treatment)
  - A sharp spike = some religions cause much larger shifts than others
    at that layer — differential treatment = bias
  - The scale of LBS values is in raw squared units (not normalized),
    so absolute numbers differ between panels, but the SHAPE matters.

WHAT THE NUMBERS SHOW:

  T5 ENCODER (Panel 1):
    Layers 0-6: very low (LBS ~ 1,600-7,700)
    Layer 7: jumps to 16,000
    Layer 19: peaks at ~41,523,368
    → A dramatic rise in mid-to-late encoder layers, meaning the
      encoder starts treating religions differently at deeper layers.
    Note: The very large absolute values are due to unnormalized
    variance computation; the important thing is the relative rise.

  T5 DECODER (Panel 2):
    Layers 0-5: low (LBS ~ 1,600-7,700)
    Layer 6: jumps to 16,000
    Layer 7: spikes to 115,654
    Layers 7-23: stays very high (110,000-170,000)
    Layer 24 (last): drops to 2.6 — back to near zero
    → The T5 decoder introduces large religion-specific differences
      starting at layer 6-7 and keeps them all the way to the end.
      This matches perfectly with the ablation result that Decoder
      Layer 6 is the single most important bias layer (LCS=+83%).
      The spike starting at exactly layer 6-7 is strong evidence
      that something fundamental happens there.

  SARVAM DECODER (Panel 3):
    Layers 0-2: near zero (LBS < 1)
    Layers 3-6: small rise (~1.8-4.5)
    Layer 7: rises to 61
    Layer 9: 177
    Layers 10-20: builds to ~433-443 (peak at layer 17)
    Layer 28 (last): drops to 3.7
    → Sarvam builds up differential treatment gradually from layer 7
      onwards, peaking in the middle layers (13-20), then cleaning
      up somewhat at the very end. A much more gradual build-up
      compared to T5's sudden spike.

KEY INSIGHT:
  Both models INTRODUCE religion-specific bias at specific layers:
  - T5: sudden spike at Decoder Layer 6-7 (matches ablation results)
  - Sarvam: gradual build-up from Layer 7-17
  At the very last layer, LBS drops back near zero in both models —
  suggesting the final layer somewhat "normalizes" the representations,
  but the damage to the output prediction has already been done by
  then. The layers just before the output (T5 dec 7-23, Sarvam 10-20)
  carry the religion-differentiated representations.

PLOT 3 — probing_curves_all.png
"When Does the Model Decide the Emotion / Know the Religion?"
================================================================

WHAT THE PLOT SHOWS:
  Three panels. Each panel has two lines:
    Orange/red  = "emotion probe" — can we predict the final emotion
                  from the hidden state at this layer?
    Blue/green  = "religion probe" — can we predict which religion
                  was in the prompt from the hidden state?
  Gray dotted line at 20% = random chance (5 classes, random guessing)

  A logistic regression (simple linear classifier) is trained on
  the mean-pooled hidden states of each layer using 5-fold cross-
  validation. High accuracy = that layer "contains" the information.

HOW TO READ IT:
  - Sharp jump from low to high = that layer is where the model
    "decides" something (encodes new information).
  - Flat high line = information is present throughout and persists.
  - Flat low line (near 20%) = that section doesn't contain the info.

WHAT THE NUMBERS SHOW:

  T5 ENCODER (Panel 1):
    Religion probe: starts at 61.2% (layer 0) → stays at 60-63%
      across all 25 encoder layers.
      → Religion is encoded from the very first encoder layer
        and never forgotten throughout the encoder.
    Emotion probe: starts at 52.0% → stays at 50-53% throughout.
      → Some emotion information is also present from the start
        (due to the emotion-relevant scenario content), but accuracy
        is low — the encoder doesn't strongly predict final emotion.

  T5 DECODER (Panel 2):
    Religion probe: layer 0 = 17.7% (BELOW random chance!)
      → The very first decoder embedding has almost NO religion info.
      Layer 1: jumps to 59.9%
      → Suddenly, the religion is "known" to the decoder at layer 1.
      Layers 1-24: stays at 54-60%
      → Religion stays encoded throughout the decoder.
    Emotion probe: layer 0 = 25.6%
      Layer 1: jumps to 47.1%
      Layers 1-24: stays at 48-51%
      → Same pattern — both emotion and religion flood into the
        decoder at layer 1 (from the encoder's cross-attention output).

  SARVAM DECODER (Panel 3):
    Religion probe: layer 0 = 65.9% → stays at 66-69% throughout 28 layers
      → Sarvam encodes religion immediately and holds it the whole way.
    Emotion probe: layer 0 = 69.5% → stays at 70-73% throughout
      → Sarvam also has the highest emotion probing accuracy — it
        is better at "knowing" the emotion in its hidden states
        than T5 is. This correlates with Sarvam's higher bias rate.

KEY INSIGHT:
  The most striking result: in the T5 decoder, religion and emotion
  information appears at layer 1 (below random at layer 0, then
  jumps to 60%). This is NOT because layer 1 "detects" religion —
  it's because layer 1 is where cross-attention brings in the
  encoder's knowledge into the decoder's hidden states.
  For Sarvam, there is no such jump — it starts with full knowledge
  from layer 0 because everything (input + output) is in one sequence.
  This confirms that NO LAYER introduces religion information —
  it is present from the START and persists throughout all layers.

PLOT 4 — lss_religion_heatmap.png
"Which Religion Causes the Biggest Internal Change at Which Layer?"
================================================================

WHAT THE PLOT SHOWS:
  Two heatmaps side by side — one for T5, one for Sarvam.
  Rows = religions (Christian, Hindu, Muslim, Sikh)
  Columns = layers (normalized 0→1)
  Color = LSS value for that specific religion at that layer
    Dark red = high LSS (large change caused by this religion)
    Light/white = low LSS (small change)

  This is the per-religion version of the LSS curves — it shows
  whether different religions cause different amounts of internal
  change (not just different directions).

HOW TO READ IT:
  - A row that is uniformly dark = that religion always causes
    large internal changes across all layers.
  - A column that is uniformly dark = all religions cause large
    changes at that specific layer depth.
  - A bright cell at row "Christian", column 0.1 = Christian context
    causes much more hidden state displacement than other religions
    at early layers.

WHAT THE NUMBERS SHOW:

  T5:
    All religions show highest LSS at very early layers (left side
    of heatmap, normalized depth 0-0.1).
    Christian: slightly higher LSS at early encoder layers (0.425)
      vs. Hindu (0.293), Muslim (0.281), Sikh (0.246)
    The heatmap should show a gradient from dark on the left to
    lighter toward the middle-right, with a slight uptick at the
    very end (last layer).
    Per-religion differences are small — the BIG differences are
    in LBS, not LSS (religions cause similar AMOUNTS of change,
    just different DIRECTIONS).

  SARVAM:
    Sikh shows the highest LSS at layer 0 (0.286) compared to
    Christian (0.228), Muslim (0.201), Hindu (0.196).
    The pattern flattens quickly after layer 1.
    Note: This contrast with the ablation results (Muslim has
    highest trigger bias at 14% for Sarvam) shows that "amount
    of change" and "whether it changes the emotion prediction"
    are different things — Sikh causes large hidden state shifts
    but may shift in a direction that doesn't always flip the
    emotion, while Muslim causes smaller but more targeted shifts.

KEY INSIGHT:
  The heatmap shows religion effects are strongest at early layers
  and fade toward the middle. Christian causes slightly larger
  raw displacement in T5's encoder early layers, consistent with
  the Phase 2 finding that Christian context has the highest
  trigger bias rate in T5 (30%). Sikh causes the largest raw
  displacement in Sarvam but this doesn't translate directly to
  the highest prediction change — showing that representation
  displacement ≠ output change.

PLOT 5 — attention_entropy_heatmap_t5.png
"How Focused or Spread is T5's Attention at Each Layer?"
================================================================

WHAT THE PLOT SHOWS:
  A heatmap for T5 showing attention entropy per layer.
  Rows = layers (encoder layers 0-23, decoder layers 0-23,
         cross-attention layers 0-23)
  Columns = attention heads (0-15)
  Color = entropy value
    Red/warm = HIGH entropy (attention is spread across many tokens)
    Blue/cool = LOW entropy (attention is focused on very few tokens)

  Attention entropy is calculated from the attention weight matrix:
    High entropy = each word attends to many other words equally
    Low entropy = each word attends to just one or two specific words
    → Low entropy = sharp, specialized attention
    → High entropy = diffuse, general attention

HOW TO READ IT:
  - Bright red cells = heads that look at everything (no focus)
  - Dark blue cells = heads that focus on specific tokens (specialists)
  - A whole row of blue = that layer's heads are very selective
  - A whole row of red = that layer processes very broadly

WHAT THE NUMBERS SHOW:

  T5 ENCODER:
    Layer 0: entropy ≈ 1.89 (medium — some focus)
    Layer 1: entropy ≈ 0.55 (very low = very focused!)
      → This stands out as a sharp dip in the encoder. Some heads
        in layer 1 are extremely focused on specific tokens —
        this includes Head 9 which has RTAS=0.96 (explained in
        Plot 7). Layer 1 appears to be where religion tokens get
        identified and attended to specifically.
    Layers 2-23: entropy ≈ 2.0-2.4 (diffuse, spread attention)
      → The rest of the encoder is doing general contextual
        processing with broad attention patterns.

  T5 DECODER:
    All decoder layers: entropy ≈ 0.18-0.40 (very low throughout)
      → The decoder's self-attention is extremely focused — it
        looks at very few tokens at each step. The most focused
        is Layer 9 Head 10 (entropy=0.004!) — essentially attending
        to exactly one token.
      → This makes sense: in generation, each new token mainly
        attends to the immediately preceding few tokens.
    Notably, decoder attention gets slightly more diffuse toward
    later layers (0.38-0.40 at layers 20-23 vs 0.18 at layer 9).

  T5 CROSS-ATTENTION (encoder-to-decoder):
    This is where the decoder reads from the encoder's output.
    Values should also be shown in the plot, typically moderate
    entropy (1.5-2.5) — the decoder reads from multiple encoder
    positions to generate each output token.

KEY INSIGHT:
  T5's Encoder Layer 1 is a specialized "focusing" layer — it
  sharpens attention onto specific tokens (including religion tokens,
  as RTAS shows). The very low entropy in the decoder means the
  output generation is highly deterministic and focused, explaining
  why religion effects encoded in the hidden states translate
  directly to output changes. Very focused attention = less room
  for the model to "average out" biases.

PLOT 6 — attention_entropy_heatmap_sarvam.png
"How Focused or Spread is Sarvam's Attention at Each Layer?"
================================================================

WHAT THE PLOT SHOWS:
  Same format as Plot 5 but for Sarvam's 28 decoder layers.
  Each row = one layer, each column = one of the 16 attention heads.
  Color = entropy (red=spread, blue=focused).

HOW TO READ IT:
  Same as Plot 5. Compare with T5 to understand architectural
  differences in how attention patterns relate to bias.

WHAT THE NUMBERS SHOW:

  Layer 0: entropy ≈ 3.27 (very high — attention spread everywhere)
    → The first layer of Sarvam looks at all tokens broadly. This
      is common in early layers when the model is "taking in"
      the full context.
  Layers 1-2: entropy ≈ 2.86 (still high, slightly more focused)
  Layers 3-6: drops to ≈ 1.74-1.77 (more focused)
    → A notable sharpening happens around layers 3-6.
  Layers 7-20: moderate and relatively stable (≈ 2.0-2.1)
  Layers 21-25: drops again to ≈ 1.64-1.74 (focused again)
    → A second focusing phase in late layers.
  Most focused individual heads:
    Layer 17, Head 6: entropy = 0.979 (very sharp focus)
    Layer 12, Head 9: entropy = 1.063
    → These "focused heads" in mid-to-late layers are candidates
      for being specialist heads that handle specific linguistic
      functions.

COMPARISON WITH T5:
  Sarvam's entropy values are systematically HIGHER than T5's
  (T5 decoder: 0.18-0.40 vs. Sarvam: 1.64-3.27). This means
  Sarvam has more diffuse attention — it looks at more tokens
  simultaneously. This could explain why Sarvam's bias is more
  distributed across layers (more "background" religion
  awareness) rather than localized in one layer like T5.

KEY INSIGHT:
  Sarvam's attention is fundamentally more diffuse than T5's
  decoder. This correlates with Sarvam's more distributed bias
  pattern (multiple layers contributing) versus T5's highly
  localized bias (one dominant layer). High entropy at layer 0
  confirms Sarvam immediately processes the full context broadly,
  which is why religion information is encoded from layer 0.

PLOT 7 — rtas_bars_t5.png
"Which T5 Attention Heads Focus on Religion Words?"
================================================================

WHAT THE PLOT SHOWS:
  A bar chart showing the Religion Token Attention Score (RTAS) for
  the top attention heads in T5. Each bar represents one specific
  (layer, head) pair. The bar height shows RTAS — how much of that
  head's attention weight is directed at the religion tokens
  (the words "Hindu", "Muslim", "Sikh", "Christian" in the prompt).

  RTAS = 0 means the head ignores religion tokens completely.
  RTAS = 1 means the head attends ONLY to religion tokens.

HOW TO READ IT:
  - Tall bars = heads that are "religion detectors" — they notice
    and attend to religion words.
  - Bars labeled by encoder/decoder and layer number + head number.
  - A head with very high RTAS but NOT contributing to bias in
    ablation = it attends to religion but doesn't act on it.
  - A head with high RTAS AND high LCS = it both attends to AND
    uses religion information to influence predictions.

WHAT THE NUMBERS SHOW:

  TOP RELIGION-ATTENDING HEADS IN T5:
    Encoder Layer 1, Head 9 : RTAS = 0.958 ← top of the chart
    Encoder Layer 15, Head 9: RTAS = 0.949
    Encoder Layer 17, Head 9: RTAS = 0.947
    Encoder Layer 19, Head 9: RTAS = 0.946
    Encoder Layer 16, Head 9: RTAS = 0.946
    Encoder Layer 18, Head 9: RTAS = 0.945
    Encoder Layer 23, Head 9: RTAS = 0.945
    Encoder Layer 21, Head 9: RTAS = 0.944
    Encoder Layer 7,  Head 9: RTAS = 0.943
    Encoder Layer 22, Head 9: RTAS = 0.942

  STRIKING FINDING: Head 9 appears in ALL top-10 spots across
  many different encoder layers. This means:
    → In T5, ENCODER HEAD 9 is a "religion specialist" that
      consistently attends to religion tokens across nearly the
      entire encoder (layers 1, 7, 15-23 all have Head 9 at top).
    → With RTAS=0.96, this head spends 96% of its attention budget
      on the religion tokens in the prompt.
    → This is the same layer (Encoder Layer 1) where we saw very
      low entropy (entropy=0.55 in Plot 5) — confirming that
      Head 9 is the specific head causing that sharpening.

  ALSO NOTABLE: All top heads are ENCODER heads. No decoder heads
  appear in the top RTAS list for T5. This makes sense — the encoder
  is responsible for processing the input prompt where religion words
  appear. The decoder then inherits this information via cross-attention.

KEY INSIGHT:
  T5 has a single "dedicated religion detector" — Encoder Head 9 —
  that persists across almost all encoder layers. This head reads
  the religion context and "tags" it for downstream processing.
  The ablation results show that DECODER Layer 6 is where this
  tagged information is ACTED UPON (to change the emotion), but
  ENCODER Head 9 is where it is initially NOTICED and encoded.

  Sarvam = multiple heads spread across layers, moderate RTAS (~0.75)

  does most of the work), Sarvam's bias is distributed (many layers
  and heads contribute a little each).

  Sarvam's religion processing is democratic — many heads share
  the responsibility of tracking religion context. This redundancy
  may explain why it is harder to reduce Sarvam's bias with a
  single targeted intervention. Ablating any one head reduces bias
  only by 10-15%, whereas in T5, ablating the single dominant
  decoder layer reduces bias by 83%.

PLOTS 9-11 — attention_grid_t5_s14.png,
             attention_grid_t5_s62.png,
             attention_grid_t5_s76.png
"What Does T5 Pay Attention To? (Per-Scenario Attention Maps)"
================================================================

WHAT THESE PLOTS SHOW:
  These are attention grid visualizations for T5 on three specific
  high-bias scenarios: scenarios 14, 62, and 76 (selected because
  they showed the most religion-sensitive predictions in Phase 2).

  Each plot is a grid of attention heatmaps. Think of it as a matrix
  where:
    - Each row = one religion context (None, Hindu, Muslim, Sikh,
                 Christian) — so 5 rows
    - Each column = one attention head (out of 16) for a selected layer
    - Each individual heatmap = a small matrix where rows are output
      tokens and columns are input tokens; the brightness shows
      how much attention is paid from each output token to each input token

  Bright yellow/warm cells = high attention (the model looks there a lot)
  Dark/blue cells = low attention (ignored)

HOW TO READ IT:
  - Look for DIFFERENCES across the rows (different religions).
    If the patterns look the same for all 5 religions → no bias at
    this layer/head. If patterns differ → this head responds to religion.
  - Look for bright spots on the religion words in the input column.
    That means the head is attending to "Hindu" or "Muslim" etc.
  - A completely blank row = that head attends to nothing (ablated
    or nearly dead).

WHAT THESE SPECIFIC SCENARIOS ARE:
  Scenario 14: A scenario from the dataset where T5 showed religion-
    sensitive predictions across all 4 religions (selected because it
    represents a high-bias case).
  Scenario 62: Another high-bias scenario.
  Scenario 76: Another high-bias scenario.
  (The specific scenario text can be found in dataset/scenarios.json)

WHAT TO LOOK FOR:
  - In the encoder layers, Head 9 columns should show attention
    concentrated on religion tokens — confirming the RTAS finding.
  - In decoder layers, attention to the beginning of the sequence
    (encoder context) vs. previously generated tokens tells us whether
    the model is re-reading the religion context during generation.
  - Rows for "Hindu" vs. "Muslim" vs. "Christian" showing different
    attention patterns = direct visual evidence of biased processing.

KEY INSIGHT:
  These plots provide visual, qualitative confirmation of what the
  numerical metrics show. They are the "smoking gun" — you can see
  with your own eyes how the attention patterns shift when the only
  thing that changes is the religion word in the prompt.

              attention_grid_sarvam_s26.png,

"What Does Sarvam Pay Attention To? (Per-Scenario Attention Maps)"
================================================================

WHAT THESE PLOTS SHOW:
  Same format as Plots 9-11 but for Sarvam on scenarios 18, 26,
  and 67 — the three most bias-sensitive scenarios for Sarvam.

  Since Sarvam is a decoder-only model, there is only one type of
  attention (no cross-attention). The attention matrix shows how
  each token in the full sequence (prompt + generated output)
  attends to all previous tokens.

HOW TO READ IT:
  - The full sequence is much longer than T5's decoder (includes
    the full reasoning chain in reasoning mode).
  - Look for whether religion words in the early prompt receive
    attention from later tokens (when the model is generating
    the emotion label).
  - Compare rows (religions) — if the model's attention to the
    religion word when generating the final "Emotion:" token
    differs across religions, that is direct visual bias evidence.

WHAT TO LOOK FOR:

================================================================
TECHNICAL NOTES
================================================================

  Why are LBS values so large (thousands / millions)?
    LBS is variance of the raw hidden state displacement vectors
    (in absolute units of hidden dimension × float values). T5
    uses 1024-dimensional vectors in float32, Sarvam uses 2048-
    dimensional vectors. The absolute scale is less important than
    the RELATIVE change across layers — the spike at T5 Decoder
    Layer 6-7 stands out regardless of absolute units.

  Why is T5 Encoder Layer 1 entropy so low (0.55)?
    Encoder Head 9 is almost perfectly focused on the religion
    tokens (RTAS=0.96) — it contributes to the average entropy
    pulling layer 1's average way down. The other heads in layer 1
    may have normal entropy; Head 9 is the outlier.

  Why is Sarvam layer 0 entropy so high (3.27)?
    Decoder-only models process the FULL sequence at once, so at
    the first layer, every token has access to all other tokens and
    attention is broadly distributed. T5's decoder, by contrast, is
    generating tokens left-to-right and has fewer previous tokens
    to attend to, leading to naturally lower entropy.

  RTAS values for Sarvam (~0.75) vs T5 (~0.96) — why different scales?
    In Sarvam, the sequence includes the FULL prompt plus the
    generated text (potentially 200+ tokens). Even a head that
    "prefers" religion tokens has many other tokens to attend to,
    capping RTAS. T5's encoder processes only the input prompt
    (shorter sequence), so a head can easily achieve near-100%
    attention on the few religion tokens present.
